## Philosophy: What a Name Actually Does

A name is not a label. It is the first handshake. Long before anyone watches a demo, lands on a website, or tries the product — they encounter the name. It either taps the spine and reverberates, or slips through the fingers and is lost.

**The name ships first.** Names travel further than features, stick longer than campaigns, and either buy credibility upfront or bleed it before you've earned the right to spend it.

A good name is a **one-word novel** — rich with suggestion, not thin and literal. It does not try to say everything. It opens a door.

**What naming is NOT:**
- Not a brainstorm on a whiteboard followed by democratic voting
- Not an AI generator prompt ("give me 10 startup names")
- Not "what's available as a .com"
- Not the last thing you do before launch

## Step 0: Intake (Do This First)

Before generating any names, understand the entity:

1. **What is it?** (one sentence — the product, not the features)
2. **Who is it for?** (specific persona, not "everyone")
3. **What emotional territory should the name own?** (3 adjectives)
4. **What should the name never feel like?** (3 anti-adjectives)
5. **What category is it in, and what names dominate that category?** (you need to know what you're near)
6. **What's the cultural context?** (dev tools? consumer? enterprise? creative?)
7. **Constraints:** Any hard requirements on domain, trademark, length?

*Do not skip intake. Names generated without context are guesses dressed as choices.*

## The Six Naming Archetypes

Every strong product name falls into one (sometimes two) of these:

### 1. Descriptive
Says what it does. Clear, low-friction, high-trust.
- Examples: Typeahead, Sidelog, Zoom, Basecamp
- Best for: B2B tools, developer tools, anything where clarity is the first job
- Risk: Forgettable, hard to trademark, implies you're only the sum of your features
- Rule: If descriptive, it must be *precise* — not generic. "Notes" is generic. "Sidelog" is precise.

### 2. Invented / Portmanteau
A new word or mashup. Distinctive, ownable, meaning-free but sound-rich.
- Examples: Noats, Figma, Spotify, Xerox, Kodak
- Best for: When you want to own a word entirely. Consumer products, brands meant to last decades.
- Risk: Requires marketing investment to attach meaning. Spell-check fights you.
- Rule: The sound must feel *right* for the emotion. Harsh consonants (K, X) feel sharp and fast. Soft sounds (N, M, L) feel warm and easy.

### 3. Metaphorical / Borrowed
Takes a word from elsewhere and imports its meaning. Rich with implication.
- Examples: Slack (engineering slack = unused capacity), Stripe (payment rails), Obsidian (dark, hard, beautiful), Notion (a notion is an idea)
- Best for: Products that benefit from a rich borrowed frame. Software especially.
- Risk: The metaphor must map cleanly — if you have to explain the connection, it's too thin.
- Rule: The best metaphorical names feel inevitable in retrospect. If it needs a paragraph to justify, it needs to go.

### 4. Cultural Resonance
A word with strong community meaning. Signals tribe membership.
- Examples: Savepoint (gaming), Softfork (dev), Impl (Rust), Hotfork (git), Patchnote (git)
- Best for: Niche products with a well-defined community. Developer tools, gaming, music.
- Risk: Excludes outsiders. Niche in/niche out.
- Rule: The cultural reference must be *known* by your target — not just cool-sounding to you.

### 5. Evocative / Abstract
A real word that sets an emotional tone without describing the product.
- Examples: Vela (sail — navigation), Mast (stability), Plume (feather — writing), Seed (foundation)
- Best for: Premium products, design-forward products, anything where feeling matters more than function
- Risk: Requires strong design and brand to anchor the emotion. Name alone floats away.
- Rule: The word must still have *resonance* — it should feel right, not random.

### 6. Founder / Legacy
Named after a person or place. Carries authority and authenticity.
- Examples: Notion (not quite, but close), Basecamp, Figma
- Best for: Professional services, high-trust categories
- Risk: Limits the brand's narrative. Hard to evolve.

## The Van Lancker Name Quality Test

A good name passes these five tests (from *How to Name Anything in an Afternoon*):

### 1. The Spine Test
Read the name aloud. Does it *do something* to you physically — a slight catch, a grin, a raised eyebrow? Or does it just sit there?

Good names tap the spine. They reverberate. They imply something before they explain anything.

### 2. The Stranger Test
Imagine a stranger hearing your name for the first time, with no context. Write down:
- What they assume the product is
- What they assume the company is like
- Whether they'd want to know more or feel nothing

If they have the right assumption — great. If they're wrong but *curious* — fine. If they feel nothing — the name is dead.

### 3. The Crowded Room Test
Say the name in a crowded room. Does it sound different from everything else? Does it have its own sound, its own phonemic identity?

Names that blend into the category sounds are invisible. SAVEPOINT in a room full of Suites, Hubs, Flows, and Clouds stands out.

### 4. The Bar Test (Van Lancker's)
Can you say "I built [name]" without embarrassment? Can you say "check out [name]" without stumbling?

This catches names that look good on paper but feel wrong in speech. Say it. Record yourself. Listen.

### 5. The Growth Test
Will the name fit in 5 years? In a different market? With a 10x bigger scope?

"Sidelog" names a developer context logger perfectly at launch. Does it name a broader developer productivity suite? Maybe not. Naming for Day 1 while thinking about Day 1000.

## Name Generation Process

### Phase 1: Territory Map (10 min)
Before generating names, map the territory you're working in:

- Write the 5 most common name patterns in the category (what are competitors called?)
- Identify 3 emotional territories you want to own
- Write 10 words from each territory — raw associations, not names yet

**The goal: don't accidentally land where everyone else already is.**

### Phase 2: Raw Generation (20 min, no editing)
Generate aggressively using these lenses:

**Lens A — The Job**
What job does the user hire this for? What words live in that job?
*"Help me think" → think, brain, mind, reason, frame, signal, draft, sketch*

**Lens B — The Metaphor**
What physical or cultural world has the same emotional shape?
*Filing → log, archive, ledger, record, trace*
*Navigation → map, chart, compass, vector, bearing, course*
*Photography → capture, frame, expose, develop, raw*

**Lens C — The Feeling**
What should it feel like to use this product?
*Fast, precise, trustworthy, calm, powerful → blade, beam, pilot, anchor, edge*

**Lens D — The Anti-Category**
What are your competitors NOT saying? Name that space.
*All competitors say "collaborate" → be alone, own, private, clear, direct*

**Lens E — Compound Mining**
Take your 3 strongest single words. Combine each with 10 common prefixes/suffixes.
Prefixes: dark-, cold-, hot-, raw-, deep-, side-, soft-, open-, clear-, fast-
Suffixes: -log, -note, -pad, -draft, -type, -mark, -line, -point, -shift, -cast

### Phase 3: Domain & Conflict Check (15 min)
For any name you're serious about:
1. DNS check: `nslookup [name].ai`, `.io`, `.com`, `.dev`
2. Trademark search: USPTO TESS, EUIPO
3. GitHub search: `github.com/[name]`
4. Web search: "[name]" in quotes — what comes up?
5. App store search: App Store + Play Store

*Directional only — always do proper legal trademark search before committing.*

### Phase 4: Persona Evaluation (20 min)
Run your top 5 names through 3-4 synthetic personas. For each name x persona:

| Metric | Scale | What It Tests |
|--------|-------|--------------|
| First Impression | 1-5 | Curiosity — does it make them want to explore? |
| Clarity | 1-5 | Do they understand what it is without explanation? |
| Memorability | 1-5 | Will they recall it tomorrow? |
| Bar Test | Yes/Maybe/No | Can they say it without cringing? |
| Quote | 1-2 sentences | What they'd actually say aloud |

Aggregate scores. Any name below 3.0 overall goes in the bin.

**Good persona coverage:** Include at least one skeptic (person who requires clarity), one early adopter (rewards distinctiveness), and one conservative buyer (needs it to be sayable in professional contexts).

### Phase 5: Final Selection Criteria
When choosing between finalists:

1. **Does the name make the category?** (best case: Slack invented a new mental category)
2. **Does the sound match the feel?** (harsh vs. soft phonetics)
3. **Is it defensible?** (trademarkable, domainable, distinct)
4. **Does the founder love it?** (they will say it 10,000 times — it must feel right to them)
5. **Does it have a story?** (names with a story travel — "we named it X because Y")

## Tagline Development

A tagline is a name's partner. The name opens the door; the tagline says what's inside.

**Tagline archetypes:**

| Type | Pattern | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| The Action | Verb + object (2-4 words) | "Just do it" / "Think different" |
| The Promise | Outcome statement | "Give your AI the deep cuts" |
| The Contrast | We do X, not Y | "Notes that never leave your machine" |
| The For | For [specific person] | "For developers who think while they code" |
| The Insight | One unexpected truth about the category | "Your name ships first" |

**Rules:**
- Under 7 words preferred
- Should work in isolation (without the product name)
- Must be testable: say it aloud to someone who doesn't know your product — do they nod?
- Avoid: "the [superlative] way to [generic verb]", "powering the future of X", anything with "seamlessly"

## Brand Voice Development

After the name, establish voice. Voice is how the name speaks.

### Voice Definition Framework:
1. **3 words that describe the voice** (e.g., "precise, direct, a little dry")
2. **3 words that the voice is NOT** (e.g., "corporate, cheerful, jargon-heavy")
3. **A passage of brand copy** written in the voice (50 words — not about the product, just the voice)
4. **The voice compass:**
   - Formal <-> Casual
   - Serious <-> Playful
   - Verbose <-> Terse
   - Technical <-> Human

### Voice for developer tools (from Noats Inc research):
The AI-native developer responds to:
- Precision — say exactly what you mean
- Directness — no marketing preamble
- Confidence — no hedging ("might", "could", "possibly")
- Authenticity — sounds like a developer, not a copywriter
- Brevity — they'll skim; every word must earn its place

## Common Naming Mistakes

### 1. Naming for Day 1 only
The name that perfectly describes your MVP may trap you in it. Think about what the product becomes, not just what it is.

### 2. Consensus-driven naming
Committees produce safe names. Safe names are invisible. One person should own the final call.

### 3. The dictionary trap
Opening a thesaurus and picking synonyms. This produces "Nimble", "Agile", "Swift", "Flow" — words that feel like names but carry no meaning.

### 4. AI generator outputs
AI generators have been trained on the same publicly available naming advice. Their outputs look plausible but lack spark, context, or wit. Use them for volume (seed your Phase 2 list) but never as final answers.

### 5. "Available" as the primary filter
Domain and trademark availability matter — but as constraints, not selection criteria. Find the right name first. Then solve the domain problem.

### 6. Untested metaphors
"It's like X for Y" naming where the metaphor doesn't land for the target audience. Always test with real personas whether the metaphor resonates.

### 7. Spelling that breaks search
Unusual spellings (Lyft, Fiverr, Tumblr) can be distinctive — but they make search harder and word-of-mouth harder. The tradeoff must be worth it.

## Domain Strategy

Priority order for tech products in 2026:
1. `.ai` — signal for AI-native products; now broadly understood; high value
2. `.com` — still the default expectation; worth buying even if not primary
3. `.io` — accepted in developer/startup culture
4. `.dev` — developer tools specifically; Google's TLD, clean signal
5. `.co` — acceptable substitute; less cluttered than `.com`

**If your preferred `.com` is taken:**
- Check if the owner is active (active website = harder to acquire; parked = negotiable)
- Consider modified variants: `get[name].com`, `use[name].com`, `[name]app.com`
- Or take a strong `.ai` or `.io` and brand around it

## Worked Examples from Noats Inc Research

### rawdraft.ai (winner — 3.92/5 overall)
- **Archetype:** Descriptive + Cultural
- **Why it won:** Maps directly to the workflow (raw thinking -> draft -> ship). 4.0 clarity — everyone understood it immediately. No false cognates.
- **Lesson:** The best names describe the *workflow*, not the product.

### sidelog.ai (runner-up — 3.67/5)
- **Archetype:** Descriptive + Metaphorical (sidecar + logging)
- **Why it works:** "Log" is dev-native. "Side" communicates non-intrusive. Together: a passive, always-on capture tool.
- **Lesson:** Two precise words beat one imprecise one.

### hotfork.ai (highest first impression — 4.0)
- **Archetype:** Cultural Resonance
- **Why it excited:** "Fork" is foundational dev vocabulary. "Hot" implies alive and active. Curiosity-maximizing.
- **Lesson:** Cultural resonance creates the highest first impression — but requires the product to deliver on the promise.

### coldfold.ai (worst score — 1.83/5)
- **Archetype:** Cultural Resonance (Haskell fold function) — too niche
- **Why it failed:** The reference only lands for functional programmers. 1.0 clarity avg.
- **Lesson:** Cultural references must be known by your *actual* target, not just cool to you.

## Quick Reference: Name Evaluation Rubric

| Score | Verdict | Action |
|-------|---------|--------|
| 4.5-5.0 | Exceptional | Protect it, move to trademark/domain immediately |
| 4.0-4.4 | Strong | Top 3 candidate, test with real humans |
| 3.5-3.9 | Good | Viable if top options unavailable; needs strong tagline |
| 3.0-3.4 | Okay | Only if all better options are unavailable |
| Below 3.0 | Don't use | Back to generation phase |

## Output Formats

When naming a product, produce one of these:

**A. Full Name Audit** (for an existing shortlist)
- Score each name on the 5-test framework
- Run through 3 synthetic personas
- Domain/conflict check
- Ranked recommendation with rationale

**B. Name Generation Sprint** (starting from scratch)
- Intake questions
- Territory map
- 30-50 raw candidates (Phases 1-2)
- Domain-checked shortlist (10-15)
- Persona-evaluated top 5
- Ranked recommendation with taglines

**C. Tagline Only**
- 10-15 tagline variants in different styles
- Grouped by archetype
- Recommended pick with rationale

**D. Brand Voice Definition**
- Voice adjectives (what it is / what it isn't)
- Sample copy passage
- Voice compass positioning
- Do/Don't list (with examples)
